Cryovolcanism refers to a form of volcanic activity that occurs on extremely cold planetary bodies, particularly icy moons in the outer solar system. Unlike terrestrial volcanism, which involves the eruption of molten rock, cryovolcanic materials are often stored beneath a frozen crust and are released through fissures or vents when internal pressure exceeds the strength of the surrounding ice layers.

One of the most well-documented examples of cryovolcanic activity has been observed on Saturn’s moon Enceladus. Data collected by spacecraft revealed jets of water vapor and ice particles erupting from fractures near the moon’s south pole. These plumes are believed to originate from subsurface reservoirs of liquid water maintained by tidal heating.
